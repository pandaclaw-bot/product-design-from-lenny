# Brain Layer: Product Positioning

Use this file as the main reasoning base for product positioning.

## 1. Positioning definition

Positioning is not a slogan. Positioning means placing a product in a customer's mind by clearly answering:
1. who it is for
2. what problem it solves
3. what category it belongs to
4. why it is better than the current alternative
5. why it matters now

## 2. Core principles

### Principle A: Positioning comes before feature lists
Users remember what a product is and why it matters, not a bag of features.

### Principle B: Borrow existing mental models first
Use a recognizable category frame before trying to invent a new category.

### Principle C: Positioning requires a switching reason
Always name the current alternative and why it is inadequate.

### Principle D: A wedge must be narrow
Prefer one painful, concrete, high-value scenario over a broad market statement.

### Principle E: A good positioning aligns product, market, and team narrative
If the team cannot prioritize around the positioning, it is still too weak.

## 3. Required lenses

### Lens 1: Target user
Make the ICP concrete and identifiable.
Bad: "all tech companies"
Good: a narrow segment with a recurring painful workflow.

### Lens 2: Core problem
Describe the cost of the current state: wasted time, risk, friction, lost revenue, compliance pain, unclear ownership, etc.

### Lens 3: Existing alternatives
Include not only direct competitors, but also spreadsheets, services, internal processes, and hacked-together tool stacks.

### Lens 4: Category frame
Answer: what kind of thing is this, in terms the market already understands?

### Lens 5: Differentiated value
Do not say merely "smarter" or "more efficient". Name what is structurally different in workflow, output quality, collaboration, or economics.

### Lens 6: Why now
Tie the product to a clear shift: technology, regulation, cost, distribution, organizational change, or workflow change.

### Lens 7: Narrative compression
A mature positioning can be compressed into one sentence without becoming generic.

### Lens 8: Expansion path
Write a wedge and a long arc. Show how the initial narrow entry expands into a broader strategic position.

## 4. AI-specific rules

If AI is involved, force answers to these:
1. Where exactly is AI embedded in the workflow?
2. Does AI create a new capability, or just a nicer interface?
3. What is automated vs assisted?
4. Why is this not replaceable by a generic model wrapper?

## 5. Anti-patterns

- broad user definitions
- positioning by feature inventory
- "AI-powered" with no specific value chain explanation
- no alternative identified
- no wedge
- no why-now
- a one-liner that sounds polished but empty

## 6. Final rubric

Only treat the positioning as mature if it is:
- clear
- narrow
- differentiated
- believable
- easy to retell
- expandable into a coherent strategy
